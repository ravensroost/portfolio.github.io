    Indigenous Peoples and Biodiversity Conservation

    For the last decade or so, a rather eye-catching statistic has been the subject of debate and discussion within the conservation and biodiversity research community. The oft-cited statistic that 80% of the planet’s remaining biodiversity is stewarded by indigenous people around the globe has been repeated by non-profits, governments, and even the World Bank and the UN. In recent years, multiple articles have poked some wide holes in the claim, pointing out just how difficult it is to define and measure ‘biodiversity’ (is it species, ecosystems, genetics, all of the above?) at a global scale, as well as determine what constitutes indigenous stewardship of it. Does it only include natural areas and species within formally recognized indigenous territories and reservations, or is it more expansive, covering lands used or inhabited by indigenous groups regardless of legal recognition?
